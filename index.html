About

Winson Hudson (1916-2004) was a courageous civil rights activist from Harmony, Mississippi, who fought tirelessly for voting rights, education, and racial justice during the Jim Crow era and beyond.

Born into a sharecropping family, she overcame poverty and segregation to become a key figure in the Mississippi Freedom Movement. Along with her husband, James "Doc" Hudson, she registered Black voters, organized community meetings, and stood against the Ku Klux Klan despite facing constant threats and intimidation. Her memoir, "Mississippi Harmony: Memoirs of a Freedom Fighter," co-written with Constance Curry, documents her extraordinary journey and the struggles of African Americans in the Deep South. Winson's unwavering commitment to justice and equality continues to inspire generations of activists fighting for civil rights.

Journey

1916

Birth & Early Years

Born into a sharecropping family in Harmony, Mississippi (Leake County). Grew up experiencing the harsh realities of Jim Crow segregation and economic exploitation in the rural South.

1940s

Marriage & Family

Married James "Doc" Hudson, who became her lifelong partner in the struggle for civil rights. Together they raised a family while building a life committed to education, community service, and fighting for equality.

1960s

Voting Rights Activism

Became a key organizer for voter registration in Leake County. Faced intimidation, economic retaliation, and threats from white supremacists, including the Ku Klux Klan. Worked with SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee) and other civil rights organizations to register Black voters despite enormous risks.

1964

Freedom Summer

Actively participated in the Mississippi Freedom Summer, hosting civil rights workers and organizing freedom schools. Her home became a meeting place for activists despite constant surveillance and threats of violence from local white supremacists.

1965-1970s

School Integration & Education

Fought for school desegregation and quality education for Black children. Worked to ensure that integration benefited Black students and teachers, challenging discrimination in newly integrated schools and protecting Black educators' jobs.

1980s-1990s

Community Leadership

Continued community organizing, working on issues of poverty, education, and economic justice. Remained active in local politics and civic engagement, mentoring younger activists and preserving the history of the civil rights movement in Mississippi.

2002

Published Memoir

Co-authored "Mississippi Harmony: Memoirs of a Freedom Fighter" with Constance Curry. The memoir documents her experiences as a civil rights activist, providing crucial first-hand testimony of the struggle for voting rights and racial justice in Mississippi.

2004

Lasting Legacy

Winson Hudson passed away, leaving behind a powerful legacy of courage, resilience, and unwavering commitment to justice. Her life story continues to inspire new generations of civil rights activists and serves as a testament to the countless unsung heroes who risked everything for freedom and equality in the American South.

Legacy

Winson Hudson's courage and determination in the face of violence and oppression helped transform Mississippi and the nation. Her work registering voters, organizing communities, and standing up to white supremacy laid the groundwork for political empowerment and social change. Through her memoir and the oral histories she shared, she ensured that the stories of grassroots activists would not be forgotten. Her legacy lives on in every person who fights for justice, every voter who exercises their hard-won rights, and every community that stands together against oppression.
